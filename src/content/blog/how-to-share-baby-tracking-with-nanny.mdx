You come home from work and ask the question: "How many times did she eat today?" Your nanny pauses. "I think... three? Maybe four. She had a big one around lunch."

That is not a bad nanny. That is a human being who spent eight hours keeping a small person alive, and the details blurred the same way they blur for you on your days at home. A nanny baby tracking app, or any shared logging system, replaces the guesswork with a record. But it only works if the person doing the logging will actually use it.

## Why verbal handoffs fail

You need to know: when was the last feed, how long naps lasted, how many diapers, anything unusual. What you typically get: "She was great today! Ate well, napped okay." That tells you the baby survived, not whether the 3 PM nap was 20 minutes or 90, whether she has eaten enough to skip the next feed, or whether the afternoon was actually fine or your nanny is just being polite.

That gap is not a communication failure. It is a format problem. Asking a person to remember timestamps from nine hours of childcare is asking them to do something you cannot do yourself. [Tracking needs a system](/blog/tracking/why-track-baby-sleep-feeds-diapers/), not just good intentions.

## What your nanny actually needs to log

Before choosing a method, get specific about what information matters. Overcomplicating the list is the fastest way to get your nanny to stop logging entirely.

**The essentials:**
- Feed time and duration (breastmilk bottle, formula, or solids)
- Volume in ounces for bottles
- Nap start and end times
- Diaper changes (wet, dirty, or both)

**Nice to have, but not required:**
- Which breast (if pumped milk)
- Mood or fussiness notes
- Tummy time or activity notes

Start with the essentials only. If your nanny is consistent with those after two weeks, add one more category. A nanny who logs four things reliably is infinitely more useful than one who was supposed to log eight and gave up on day three.

## Three ways your nanny can log

The method needs to work for the person doing the tracking, not for you. Your nanny will be logging events while a baby is crying, eating, or about to need something. The method has to survive that reality.

### Paper log on the counter

Print a daily sheet with columns for time, event type, and notes. Tape it to the fridge. No setup, no learning curve, no app store. Some nannies love it. It feels low-pressure.

Where it breaks down: you come home and see "bot 3oz" in the 2 PM slot and nothing after that. Did the baby not eat again, or did your nanny forget to log the 4:30 bottle? Paper has no timestamps unless the nanny writes them, no totals unless someone does the arithmetic, and you only see it when you are physically in the kitchen.

### Shared phone app

You share access to a baby tracking app, and both of you see the same data in real time. Feeds, naps, diapers, all timestamped and totaled. You can check from your desk at lunch and know exactly how the day is going. If your nanny logs consistently, an app gives you more insight than any handoff conversation ever could.

Where it breaks down: setup friction. Your nanny needs to download the app, create an account, and learn the interface. Plenty of caregivers, especially older nannies or family members who help part-time, are not comfortable navigating a new app. And every time the nanny needs to log something, she is unlocking a phone, splitting attention between the baby and a screen.

### Physical tracker on the counter

A dedicated device sits in the nursery or on the kitchen counter. Your nanny taps a button to log a feed, a nap, or a diaper. The device syncs to a companion app over Bluetooth, so every event appears on your phone in real time. No separate login for the nanny. No app to install on her phone. No training beyond "press this button."

Where it breaks down: the nanny cannot add detailed notes from the device itself. If you need to know that the baby refused the bottle and only ate 2 of the 4 ounces, that detail has to come verbally or through the app. The device handles the "what happened and when" part. The nuance still comes from conversation.

### Comparison at a glance

| | Paper log | Shared app | Physical tracker |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Nanny effort** | Medium (pen, remember to write) | Medium-High (learn app, remember to log) | Low (one tap) |
| **Parent visibility** | Low (only when home) | High (real-time from anywhere) | High (syncs to parent's app) |
| **Real-time sync** | No | Yes | Yes |
| **Learning curve** | None | Moderate | Almost none |
| **Works with tech-averse caregivers** | Yes | Not always | Yes |

## How to pick the right method for your household

The best tracking system for a multi-caregiver household is the one the caregiver will actually use without being reminded. That is it. That is the entire decision framework.

**Pick paper if** your nanny prefers it, you are home most of the time and can read the log same-day, and you do not need real-time visibility. It is also a good bridge for the first week while your nanny settles in.

**Pick a shared app if** your nanny is comfortable with technology, you want real-time data while you are away, and both of you are willing to commit to the habit. This gives you the richest data.

## Setting up the handoff conversation

Before your nanny's first day with the new system, have a five-minute conversation. Not a training session. A conversation.

1. **Explain what you track and why.** "We log feeds, naps, and diapers so the pediatrician has real numbers, and so I can pick up where you left off without asking you twenty questions."
2. **Show the system once.** If it is an app, walk through it together. If it is a device, demonstrate the tap. If it is paper, show where the sheet lives and what the columns mean.
3. **Say this out loud:** "If you forget to log something, it is fine. Just estimate the time when you remember. An imperfect log is better than no log."
4. **Ask what would make it easier for her.** You might learn the device needs to be in the kitchen instead of the nursery, or that she would rather use voice logging through Alexa while she is warming a bottle.

The goal is buy-in, not compliance. A nanny who understands why the tracking matters will log more consistently than one who feels surveilled.

## The first week reality check

Your nanny will not log everything perfectly on day one. That is normal. Look at the data after the first week and ask: are the big-picture patterns visible? If yes, the system is working. If half the day is blank, the method is too cumbersome and you need to simplify.

The benchmark is not perfection. The benchmark is: when the [pediatrician asks how many ounces she had yesterday](/blog/feeding/newborn-feeding-schedule/), can you answer with a number instead of "I think our nanny said it was a good day"?

## It gets easier

The first two weeks are the hardest. Your nanny is learning the baby, the baby is adjusting, and you are adjusting to not being the one in charge during the day.

But once the habit clicks, usually by week two or three, you stop having those anxious "how was today?" conversations entirely. You already know. You saw the log at lunch. You know she napped for 90 minutes, ate 15 ounces, and had her normal diaper count. The evening handoff becomes a real conversation about the baby instead of a forensic reconstruction of the day's events.
