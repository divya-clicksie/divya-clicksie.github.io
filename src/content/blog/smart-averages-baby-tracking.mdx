You tracked six bottles on Monday. Five on Tuesday. Wednesday, grandma was babysitting and nobody opened the app. Thursday you were back on it with six again. Friday, five. Saturday, four logged but you know she had at least two more while you were napping. Sunday, six.

Your baby tracker adds it all up and shows a baby feeding average per day of 5.4 bottles. Divide 38 by seven. Simple math. Except the number is wrong.

That number is wrong. And if you bring it to your pediatrician, it could lead to a conversation about insufficient intake that your baby does not actually have.

## Why simple averages lie

The math behind most baby tracker averages is basic: total events divided by total days. It treats every day equally, whether you logged every feed or missed half of them.

The problem is obvious once you see it. Wednesday's zero bottles and Saturday's four logged bottles are pulling the average down, but your baby did not actually eat less on those days. You just did not track it. The data has gaps, and a simple average treats a gap like a fact.

This matters most for feeding. Your pediatrician wants to know how much your baby eats per day. If the number on your screen is dragged down by a chaotic Saturday when the app stayed in your pocket, it stops being useful. Worse, it becomes misleading.

## How Smart Averages fix the math

Smart Averages work by identifying which days have complete (or nearly complete) data and which do not. Here is the logic:

1. **Find your peak day.** Look at the last seven days and find the day with the highest logged count. That is your best-tracked day and becomes the reference point.
2. **Set a threshold.** Any day with fewer than 80% of the peak day's count gets flagged as incomplete.
3. **Average only the robust days.** The incomplete days are excluded from the calculation entirely.

The result is an average built from the days you actually tracked, not the days you forgot.

## A week in practice

Here is what this looks like with real numbers:

| Day | Bottles logged | Status |
|-----|---------------|--------|
| Monday | 6 | Complete |
| Tuesday | 5 | Complete |
| Wednesday | 0 | Incomplete |
| Thursday | 6 | Complete |
| Friday | 5 | Complete |
| Saturday | 4 | Incomplete |
| Sunday | 6 | Complete |

The peak day is 6 bottles. The 80% threshold is 4.8, so any day with fewer than 5 logged bottles gets flagged. Wednesday (0) and Saturday (4) are excluded.

**Simple average:** 38 / 7 = **5.4 bottles/day**
**Smart Average:** 28 / 5 = **5.6 bottles/day**

That 0.2-bottle difference does not look dramatic in a table. But scale it over a month with several incomplete days, and the gap widens. A naive average might show 4.8 bottles when the real number is 5.5. That is the difference between "looks fine" and "let's talk about supplementing."

## The real number matters at the pediatrician

When your doctor asks "how much is she eating per day?" they want the actual intake, not a number distorted by your logging consistency. They cannot adjust for the fact that grandma did not open the app. Smart Averages make that adjustment for you.

The same logic applies to sleep totals. If you forgot to log the morning nap on two days this week, a simple average says your baby sleeps less than she does. Smart Averages recognize those days are under-reported and set them aside.

## Gaps happen less when logging is easier

Missed days happen to everyone. But they happen less when logging is a single tap on a device sitting on the counter instead of unlocking your phone, finding the app, and navigating to the right screen. Smart Averages are the safety net for when gaps still occur, because even with the simplest system, life gets in the way sometimes.

The goal is not perfect data. It is accurate insight from imperfect data. One chaotic day should not wreck the number your pediatrician sees, and now it does not.
